MODULE 1: Introduction to Statistics

1. Definition & Overview

  • Statistics: The scientific discipline concerned with the collection, organization, analysis, and interpretation of data.

  • The 4 Phases of Statistics:

    1. Collection: Gathering raw data (e.g., surveys, experiments, observations).

    2. Presentation: Organizing data into tables, graphs, or charts to make it readable.

    3. Analysis: Extracting relevant trends, patterns, and significant information.

    4. Interpretation: Drawing conclusions and making decisions based on the analysis.

2. The Two Main Branches

  • Descriptive Statistics:

    • Organizes, summarizes, and displays data.

    • Focus: What is happening? (e.g., Average score, most popular choice).

  • Inferential Statistics:

    • Uses sample data to draw conclusions or predictions about a larger population.

    • Focus: What might happen? (e.g., Predicting election results based on a poll).

3. Types of Data

  • Categorical (Qualitative): Describes qualities or characteristics.

    • Examples: Sex, Religion, Brand preference, Yes/No.

  • Numerical (Quantitative): Describes quantities (numbers).

    • Discrete: Countable whole numbers (e.g., Number of children, Absences).

    • Continuous: Measurable values that can have decimals (e.g., Height, Weight, Temperature).

4. Levels of Measurement (NOIR)

  1. Nominal: Names/Labels only. No inherent order. (e.g., Gender, Blood Type).

  2. Ordinal: Names + Order/Rank matters. (e.g., Likert Scale, 1st/2nd/3rd place).

  3. Interval: Order + Equal Intervals. No absolute zero. (e.g., Temperature in Celsius—0°C does not mean “no heat”).

  4. Ratio: Order + Intervals + Absolute Zero exists. (e.g., Height, Weight, Money—0 means none).

MODULE 2: Statistical Assumptions

Before choosing a statistical test, you must analyze the nature of your data.

1. Key Assumptions

  • Normality: Data should follow a symmetrical Normal Distribution (Bell Curve).

    • Tests: Shapiro-Wilk, Kolmogorov-Smirnov.

  • Homoscedasticity: The variance (spread) between groups being compared must be equal.

    • Test: Levene’s Test.

  • Independence: Observations in one group should not influence the other (unless using paired samples).

2. Choosing the Right Test

  • Parametric Tests:

    • Use when: Data is Normal and assumptions are met.

    • Characteristics: More powerful, stricter rules.

  • Non-Parametric Tests:

    • Use when: Data is NOT Normal (Skewed) or assumptions are violated.

    • Characteristics: Robust to outliers, less strict (“Safe fallback”).

MODULE 3: Data Organization & Presentation

1. Ways to Organize Data

  • Array: Arranging raw numerical data in ascending or descending order.

  • Stem-and-Leaf Diagram: Displays data by separating the leading digit (stem) from the trailing digit (leaf). Preserves individual values.

  • Frequency Distribution Table (FDT): Groups large datasets into specific Class Intervals to show how often values occur.

2. Ways to Present Data

  • Textual Presentation: Uses paragraphs/sentences to explain data (Narrative report).

  • Tabular Presentation: Uses rows and columns for precise data lookup.

  • Graphical (Visual) Presentation:

    • Bar Graph: Comparing quantities across different categories (Discrete data).

3. Measures of Shape (The Pattern)

  • Skewness: Measures symmetry.

    • Normal: 0 (Symmetrical).

    • Positive Skew: Tail extends to the Right (Low scores are common).

    • Negative Skew: Tail extends to the Left (High scores are common).

  • Kurtosis: Measures peakedness/tailedness.

    • Leptokurtic (>3>3>3 ): High peak, heavy tails (More outliers).

    • Mesokurtic (=3=3=3 ): Normal bell shape.

    • Platykurtic (<3<3<3 ): Flat peak, light tails (Fewer outliers).

4. Interpreting Findings

  • p-value < 0.05: Significant. (Reject the Null Hypothesis).

  • p-value > 0.05: Not Significant. (Accept the Null Hypothesis).

  • Important Note: Correlation does not imply causation. Just because two things are related doesn’t mean one caused the other.

MODULE 6: Ethical Use of Data

1. Definition

  • Ethics: The discipline dealing with what is morally good and bad, and right and wrong.

2. Core Ethical Principles

  • Respect for Persons: Treating individuals with autonomy and dignity.

  • Beneficence & Non-Maleficence: Maximizing benefits and minimizing harm.

  • Justice & Fairness: Ensuring equitable distribution of benefits and risks.

  • Transparency & Accountability: Being open about methods, funding, and findings.

3. Informed Consent

Participants must understand the study before agreeing.

  • Clear purpose.

  • Voluntary participation (no coercion).

  • Right to withdraw at any time.

  • No deception.

4. Privacy & Confidentiality

  • Data Privacy Act (RA 10173): Legal framework protecting personal data.

  • Best Practices: Anonymize datasets, use encryption, and collect only necessary data.

5. Ethical Data Analysis

  • Avoid Manipulation: Do not alter data to fit a desired outcome (“p-hacking”).

  • Handle Outliers Properly: Investigate them; do not just delete them to clean the curve.

  • Report Limitations: Be honest about what the study didn’t cover or where it might be weak.
